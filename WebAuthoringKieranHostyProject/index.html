The Summary

Back in the good old days, before nuclear test ban treaties...

Its Origins

In 1958, a group of researchers working for General Atomic (nuclear subdivision of General Dynamics) conducted a study into the effectiveness of multiple nuclear pulses as a method of propulsion into space. 

The results were surprisingly effective, being a system that scales up better the larger you make the overall rocket 

Reality Catches Up

Nuclear Test Ban treaties, fallout concerns (mostly unfounded), and concerns with modern technology regard the Electro-Magnetic-Pulses (EMPs) emitted from nuclear chain-reactions all contribute to the death of the Orion Program around 1963.     

Good Ideas Don't Die

For when you need high performance for every kilogram, few things beat a box of uranium isotopes.

Medusa, Mini-Mag Orion, Daedalus, Longshot and even Magneto-Inertial Fusion, Fission and Fusion are not leaving the toolbox.

Project Daedalus

Fusion is the favourite kid of everyone and for some good reasons.

Cheaper, more abundant, easier to work with (theoretically), starred in that second back to the future movie...   
Magneto-Inertial Fusion  

Fusion turns out to be REALLY difficult, creating the entire field of plasma physics, so someone decided to crush the hydrogen with collapsing magnetic foils. Can't be hindered by plasma if the plasma is stuck in a box.

Nuclear Electric

Fission reactors can generate enough energy on their own, without needing to be a runaway reaction, so someone sensible in NASA figured that they should just use a nuclear generator on board a rocket to power an electric drive that propells Xenon ions with poorer acceleration, but much higher efficiency. This is the most acceptable form of nuclear propulsion currently, and nuclear generators already power the martian rovers. 

Nuclear Thermal

This is a less efficient form of propulsion than the nuclear electric drive, but it has very considerable advantages in its ability to create significant amounts of thrust, and at a much higher efficiency than the most powerful chemical engines in use today. (All engines that are used to propell something into orbit are currently chemical engines, so this would be an absolute game-changer that could send humanity into a science-fiction future straight out of the Expanse series if it is accepted.)
